# Default Organizational Chart

This document outlines the standard organizational structure for newly onboarded companies. It serves as a foundational blueprint for employee onboarding, role assignment, reporting structure mapping, and integration with HR & Payroll workflows.

---

## A. Hierarchical Org Chart

```text
[Company Name]
 │
 ├── Leadership
 │    └── Chief Executive Officer (CEO) / Founder
 │
 ├── Human Resources (HR)
 │    ├── HR Director / Manager
 │    └── HR Generalist / Recruiter
 │
 ├── Finance
 │    ├── Chief Financial Officer (CFO) / Finance Director
 │    └── Accountant / Financial Analyst
 │
 ├── Engineering / Tech
 │    ├── Chief Technology Officer (CTO) / VP of Engineering
 │    ├── Engineering Manager
 │    └── Software Engineer (Front-end / Back-end / Full-stack)
 │
 ├── Sales
 │    ├── VP of Sales / Sales Director
 │    ├── Sales Manager
 │    └── Account Executive / Sales Representative
 │
 ├── Marketing
 │    ├── Chief Marketing Officer (CMO) / Marketing Director
 │    ├── Marketing Manager
 │    └── Content Strategist / Marketing Specialist
 │
 └── Operations
      ├── Chief Operating Officer (COO) / VP of Operations
      ├── Operations Manager
      └── Operations Specialist / Administrator
```

## D. HR Expansion Notes & Customization

This structure is designed to be flexible. HR Administrators can modify it as the company grows:

1. **Adding/Removing Departments:** New departments (e.g., Customer Success, Product, Legal) can be added as top-level nodes under the CEO, or as sub-departments (e.g., QA under Engineering). Unused departments can be hidden or removed.
2. **Adding Custom Roles:** Create specialized roles (e.g., "Data Scientist" under Engineering, or "Payroll Specialist" under Finance) as needed.
3. **Moving Positions:** If organizational restructuring occurs (e.g., Marketing reporting to Sales temporarily), reporting lines can be updated directly in the HRMS.
4. **Vacant Roles:** Positions can be created before they are filled. This helps with headcount planning and recruitment tracking. Employees can be assigned to these vacant slots at a later date.
5. **Cross-functional Reporting:** While the primary hierarchy is strict, matrix reporting (dotted lines) can be configured for project-based management if required by the HRMS settings.

## E. Best Practices & Common Mistakes

### Best Practices for Org Structuring:
* **Span of Control:** Aim for a balanced span of control. Usually, 5-8 direct reports per manager is optimal. Too many reports lead to burnout; too few lead to micromanagement.
* **Clear Chains of Command:** Ensure every employee has exactly one direct manager for HR and payroll approval purposes.
* **Role clarity over Title inflation:** Focus on clearly defining the responsibilities of a position rather than creating overly complex or inflated titles.
* **Scalability:** Design the structure not just for today, but for where the company expects to be in 12-18 months.

### Common Mistakes to Avoid:
* **The "Flat" Trap:** Avoiding hierarchy entirely as the company grows often leads to chaos, unclear decision-making, and overwhelmed founders.
* **Orphaned Roles:** Creating roles that don't clearly report to anyone, leading to lack of guidance and accountability.
* **Designing Around People:** Try to design the org chart around the *functions* the business needs, rather than creating special roles just to fit current specific individuals.
* **Forgetting Support Functions:** Early-stage companies often neglect formalizing Operations and HR until it's too late. Having these in the foundational structure ensures they aren't forgotten.
